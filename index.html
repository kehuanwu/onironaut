Belle de Jour (1967)

Luis Bunuel

Catherine Deneuve, Jean Sorel

Séverine Serizy, a young, beautiful housewife who is emotionally distant from her husband and struggles with unfulfilled desires. She leads a double life, secretly working at a high-class brothel during the afternoons, adopting the pseudonym "Belle de Jour." While at the brothel, she explores her fantasies and hidden desires in a world of sexual adventure. Her two lives remain separate, but as her psychological conflicts grow, the lines between her reality and fantasies begin to blur, leading to a dramatic confrontation of her inner world with her outward life.

The film plays heavily with dreamlike sequences and nonlinear storytelling, particularly in its use of fantasy and daydreams to reveal Séverine’s subconscious desires. Buñuel blurs the boundaries between Séverine's real life and her imagined fantasies, making it difficult to distinguish between what is real and what is a projection of her mind. The film’s structure reflects the fluidity of dreams, where fantasy invades reality and time seems to lose its coherence.

The Cell (2000)

Tarsem Singh

Jennifer Lopez, Vince Vaughn

A psychotherapist, Dr. Catherine Deane, uses experimental technology to enter the minds of her patients, exploring their subconscious worlds to help them recover from trauma. She is called upon to enter the mind of a comatose serial killer, Carl Stargher, in order to locate his latest victim, who is still alive but hidden in an unknown location. Inside Carl’s mind, Catherine navigates a terrifying and surreal landscape, shaped by his violent and traumatic past. As the lines between Carl’s mental world and reality blur, Catherine risks losing herself in the depths of his subconscious while racing against time to save the victim.

The technology that allows Catherine to explore these dreamlike landscapes turns the film into a psychological journey, where time and space lose their conventional meaning. By exploring the subconscious in such a visually striking and disjointed way, the film challenges linear storytelling and immerses the audience in a world where the subconscious mind dictates the flow of the narrative, much like the unpredictable logic of dreams.

Bi Gan
